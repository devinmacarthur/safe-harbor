# Fairness Principles Analysis

## The Core Dispute

Two valid but conflicting views of ownership:

```
Mae's frame:  "My family brought $100k. That should count forever."
Starseed's frame: "I'm paying half the mortgage for 30 years. My equity should grow."
```

Both perspectives are legitimate. The challenge is designing a structure that honors both.

---

## Mae's Perspective (Parents' Contribution Side)

### Underlying Needs

| Need | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Recognition** | The $100k was real money with real opportunity cost |
| **Protection** | Family capital should be safeguarded |
| **Acknowledgment of Risk** | Parents trusted Mae; that trust has weight |
| **No Free Riding** | Starseed shouldn't benefit "unfairly" from family money |

### Emotional Risks if Unmet

| Risk | Consequence |
|------|-------------|
| Feels "erased" | Parents' sacrifice ignored or minimized |
| Family resentment | Parents feel their money was "lost" to Starseed |
| Defensive posture | Every future discussion becomes a fight |
| Scorekeeping | Passive-aggressive tension in the relationship |

### What Mae Needs to Hear

- "I want your parents to feel protected"
- "The $100k should be returned first"
- "I'm not trying to take what isn't mine"

---

## Starseed's Perspective (Ongoing Payer Side)

### Underlying Needs

| Need | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Growing Ownership** | Equity should build from consistent payments |
| **Not Permanent Minority** | Shouldn't be second-class owner forever |
| **Long-term Effort Counts** | Years of payments should matter |
| **Fairness** | Shouldn't be punished for not having wealthy parents |

### Emotional Risks if Unmet

| Risk | Consequence |
|------|-------------|
| Feels like a "renter" | Paying mortgage but not building equity |
| Resentment builds | Years of payment with no recognition |
| Feels trapped | Can't build wealth no matter what |
| Class tension | Privilege disparity poisons friendship |

### What Starseed Needs to Hear

- "Your payments are building real equity"
- "You're not locked into permanent minority"
- "Your long-term commitment matters"

---

## The Timing vs. Risk Tension

The fundamental question: **How do you account for timing and risk?**

### Mae's Position (Emphasizes Timing)
- $100k upfront = higher risk
- Early money is more valuable (time value)
- Parents could have invested elsewhere
- Without down payment, no house at all

### Starseed's Position (Emphasizes Duration)
- 30 years of payments = sustained commitment
- Interest goes to bank, not equity
- Principal payments build ownership
- Long-term reliability should count

### The Bridge

A good model acknowledges BOTH:
1. Upfront capital deserves protection and recognition
2. Ongoing payments deserve equity accumulation
3. These don't have to be in conflict

---

## Principles for a Fair Agreement

### Principle 1: Protect the Downpayment
The $100k should be returned before profits are split. This protects Mae's parents without giving them appreciation rights.

### Principle 2: Equity Grows from Principal
Ownership shares should reflect who actually paid down the mortgage, not just who made payments (interest doesn't build equity).

### Principle 3: Track Everything
Improvements, extra payments, and missed payments should all be documented and reflected in the final split.

### Principle 4: Handle Edge Cases
The agreement should address: early sale, market decline, one party can't pay, one party wants out, death/disability.

### Principle 5: Simple Enough to Explain
Both parties should be able to explain the arrangement to a friend in under 2 minutes.

### Principle 6: Preserve the Friendship
The model should minimize opportunities for resentment and scorekeeping.
