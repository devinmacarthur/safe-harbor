# Meeting Facilitation Guide

## For Starseed: How to Have This Conversation with Mae

---

## Conversation Opening Script

Use this to start the conversation:

---

**"Hey Mae, I wanted to talk about something important -- not because anything is wrong, but because I think we should be proactive about our house situation.**

**When we bought the place, your parents were incredibly generous with the down payment. I'm grateful for that. And we've both been paying the mortgage without any issues.**

**But we never actually wrote down how we'd handle things like: what if one of us wants to sell someday? What if the house goes up or down in value? What if one of us needs to move for work?**

**I've been thinking about this, and I realized we probably have different assumptions in our heads about how equity works between us. I'd rather talk about it now, when everything is fine, than wait until there's pressure.**

**Can we set aside some time to work through this together? I've done some research on different models, and I want to find something that feels fair to both of us.**

**This isn't about me trying to take anything -- it's about making sure we stay good friends by being clear upfront."**

---

## Values Alignment Questions

Ask these to understand what Mae actually cares about:

### Question 1
**"When you think about your parents' contribution, what feels most important to protect?"**

*Listen for:* return of capital vs. return on capital vs. appreciation share

### Question 2
**"If we sold in 5 years and made $100k profit, how would you instinctively want to split it?"**

*Listen for:* their mental model of fairness

### Question 3
**"What would feel unfair to you?"**

*Listen for:* their fears and red lines

### Question 4
**"How do you think about my monthly payments building equity over time?"**

*Listen for:* whether they see payments as rent or ownership

### Question 5
**"If your parents asked about the arrangement, what would you want to be able to tell them?"**

*Listen for:* family pressure points

## Red Flag Phrases to Avoid

### DON'T SAY

| Phrase | Why It's Dangerous |
|--------|-------------------|
| "Your parents' money shouldn't count as much as..." | Dismisses their contribution |
| "I'm basically paying rent if..." | Creates adversarial framing |
| "That's not fair to me" | Invites "well, this isn't fair to ME" |
| "I looked it up and legally..." | Feels like a threat |
| "If we don't agree, I could..." | Ultimatum mode |
| "You're getting a free ride because..." | Accusatory |

### DO SAY

| Phrase | Why It Works |
|--------|--------------|
| "Help me understand how you see it" | Invites their perspective |
| "I want your parents to feel protected" | Shows good faith |
| "What would feel fair to both of us?" | Collaborative framing |
| "I've thought about a few options..." | Positions as problem-solving |
| "We're both taking risk here" | Acknowledges shared stakes |
| "Let's write something down so we don't have to remember" | Practical, not emotional |

## Common Trigger Points

### Where People Usually Get Defensive

#### 1. When you imply the down payment "doesn't count"
- Mae may hear: "Your parents' sacrifice means nothing"
- **Instead say:** "I want to make sure the $100k is protected AND that I'm building equity too"

#### 2. When you bring up specific numbers
- "I've paid $X so far" can feel like scorekeeping
- **Instead say:** "Let's build a system that tracks automatically"

#### 3. When you mention what happens if you split
- Can feel like you're planning to leave
- **Instead say:** "We should plan for all scenarios, not because we expect them"

#### 4. When you suggest different ownership percentages
- "50/50 isn't fair" sounds like an attack
- **Instead say:** "What structure matches how we both contributed?"

#### 5. When family is mentioned
- "Your parents" can trigger defensiveness
- **Instead say:** Use "the down payment" or "the initial contribution"

## If Mae Pushes Back

### If Mae says: "Ownership should be based on total cash, period."

**Response:** "I understand that perspective. Let me show you what that looks like over time... [show Model 1 numbers]. My concern is that even after paying for 10 years, I'd still be under 40%. Does that feel right to you? What if we found a middle ground?"

### If Mae says: "My parents took all the risk upfront."

**Response:** "They did, and I want that protected. What if we structured it so they get their $100k back first before any split? That way their money is safe, and I still build equity from my ongoing payments."

### If Mae says: "We're friends, we don't need a contract."

**Response:** "We're friends, and that's exactly why I want to do this. Contracts aren't for when things are good -- they're for when things get hard. A clear agreement means we never have to guess or argue about who assumed what."

### If Mae says: "Let's just figure it out when we sell."

**Response:** "I hear you, but by then we might have very different memories of what we agreed to. Writing it down now, while we're on the same page, is a gift to our future selves."

### If Mae says: "I need to talk to my parents about this."

**Response:** "That makes total sense. Take the time you need. Maybe it would help if I wrote up a summary of the options so you can show them? I want them to feel good about this too."

## Meeting Structure Suggestion

### Meeting 1: Values and Concerns (45-60 min)
- Share this analysis
- Ask alignment questions
- Identify areas of agreement and disagreement
- **No decisions yet** -- just listen and understand

### Meeting 2: Model Selection (60-90 min)
- Review all four models with your actual numbers
- Discuss which feels right to both of you
- Draft key terms together
- Identify any items needing more thought

### Meeting 3: Final Review (30 min)
- Review draft term sheet
- Make final adjustments
- Agree to take to attorney together

## Signs the Conversation Is Going Well

- Mae asks clarifying questions
- Mae acknowledges your perspective
- Mae suggests modifications rather than rejecting outright
- Mae says "that's fair" or "I hadn't thought about it that way"
- Mae wants to involve parents in a constructive way

## Signs to Pause and Regroup

- Mae becomes defensive or dismissive
- Conversation becomes about "winning"
- Either party raises voice or uses accusatory language
- Mae says "I need to think about this" (respect it -- don't push)
- You feel yourself getting frustrated

**If things get tense:** "I can tell this is bringing up some feelings for both of us. Let's take a break and come back to this when we've both had time to think."

---

## The Goal

The goal is NOT to "win" the negotiation.

The goal is to create a structure where:
- Mae's parents feel their generosity is protected
- Starseed builds meaningful equity over time
- Both parties understand exactly what they're agreeing to
- The friendship survives regardless of what happens to the house

## Critical Pre-Conversation Step

**Before having this conversation, you need clarity on what Mae's parents actually expect.**

The closing documents list the $100k as "Gift Funds" for mortgage purposes, but families often have informal expectations.

**Suggested question for Mae:**

"Have your parents ever said anything about whether they expect the $100k back someday, or if it was truly a gift to us?"

This is the #1 risk factor. If parents expect:
- **Nothing:** Model 3 (Waterfall) works perfectly
- **Return of principal only:** Model 3 handles this
- **Return + interest:** Need to modify Layer 1
- **Share of appreciation:** Changes everything -- they become partners

Get clarity on this FIRST.
